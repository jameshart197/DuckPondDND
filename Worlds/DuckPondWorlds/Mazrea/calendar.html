Calendar

The Calendar of Mazrea is made up of a 10 month year, each month consisting of 30 days, divided up into 3 weeks of 10 days each. This calendar has been in use for about 1500 years, and is based upon the 4 moons of Mazrea; Gaecilia, Jurus, Borlean and Quan. Gaecilia’s even 30 day cycle gives basis for the months, and the journey around the Central Sun takes 300 days, giving format to the calendar.

With Gaecilia on a 30 day cycle, Jurus a 60 day cycle, Borlean a 120 day cycle and Quan on a 10 day cycle, nights on which the sky is “Two Moons Bright” are fairly common (when two moons are full or near-full in the sky), “Three Moons Bright” are fairly common, occurring two to three times a month and “Four Moons Bright” very rare. Four Moons Bright nights are celebrated throughout the world of Mazrea in various different ways. With the cycles of the moons, a night where no moon is in the sky is almost impossible, though nights where all the moons are dim are thought of as unlucky by most of Mazrea.

Festivals of Mazrea

Whilst there are many festivals that occur on a smaller scale (either in a particular village, town or even country), there are a few festivals that are celebrated worldwide. These are as follows;

Also known as Wintersong or The Day of Gifting in places that do not follow or worship the Constellation, the holiday is celebrated throughout the world as a day of family and friendship, and a day to give to those you love. Wintersong gifts can be lavish or simple, but it is considered extremely bad luck to give no gifts at all on the Night of the Evenstar. Gifts are traditionally given at sunset (usually fairly early in the day during this time of year) and throughout the day feasts and celebrations are held. After the gifting, it is tradition to light small candles and place them in holders, lanterns or containers at the door of your home, to show love and joy to the Evenstar.

Believed to be the day on which Dragons entered the world, the Day of Dragons is one of the oldest festivals celebrated throughout the world. On this day there are traditional parades and many light lanterns or send lights to the sky for the Dragons to see. It is also common to exchange or tell fortunes on this day. It is often a day for music and dancing.

Whilst celebrated worldwide, traditions vary from country to country and place to place; Much of the observance of the Solstice involves being present at sunrise and/or sunset and giving thanks for the day, and will often involve a meal shared amongst family and friends.

The evening on which it is believed that the barrier between the Fugue Plane and the Material Plane is at its lowest, ghosts and spirits who struggle to display physical manifestation will often appear solely on this day. From family ghosts to the haunted spirits of ancient barrows, the departed are often observed on this long evening. From macabre celebrations of death and haunting to gentle festivals of candles, observation of this festival changes from place to place.

Often referred to as the Festival of the Departing, the Long Farewell is a night not only to say goodbye to those that have departed but also to bid farewell to the year as whole. This festival is one of the few observed worldwide where the tradition remains the same in all places; a simple bonfire, where each member of the town, village, community or home lays a piece of firewood on the flames. This is seen as putting the departed, painful memories, the year and troubles to rest, to burn to ashes and rise as the new day.

A day for sharing with those in need, this festival varies slightly differently depending on your location, but always revolves around sharing food or other necessities with those that have less. In much of Kelaan, things that you can spare are placed outside the home, for those in need to collect at their own will. In Gallania this is often a feast within the centre of the town or city for all to come, where those in need are gifted items. Elsewhere in the world, customs may vary but the theme remains the same.
